# MASTER DEEP CHECKLIST FOR THE WHOLE PROJECT

**A. Project identity and intellectual coherence**
- [ ] The project has one central educational problem, not several unrelated ones.
- [ ] The problem is clearly stated as a gap in academic writing assessment and support.
- [ ] The project is explicitly positioned as pedagogy-driven and AI-supported, not technology-driven.
- [ ] The title, objectives, research questions, methodology, AI system, and expected contribution all refer to the same core problem.
- [ ] The project shows one consistent logic: academic writing difficulty → learner data collection → diagnostic interpretation → adaptive feedback → revision → measurable improvement
- [ ] The study is framed as an integrated architecture, not as four parallel topics: writing assessment, learning analytics, adaptive pedagogy, AI/Bayesian modelling.
- [ ] The project clearly explains why this study matters in EFL higher education.
- [ ] The project clearly explains why Moodle data matters pedagogically, not just technically.
- [ ] The project clearly explains why academic writing must be assessed both as process and product.
- [ ] The project clearly explains why AI is included and what its role is.
- [ ] The project clearly explains why AI is limited and why teacher supervision remains essential.

**B. Problem statement and research gap**
- [ ] The problem statement identifies real weaknesses of traditional writing assessment: summative, delayed, generalized, non-adaptive, non-data-informed.
- [ ] The research gap is clearly articulated: limited integration of learning analytics and writing pedagogy, weak use of behavioural data in EFL academic writing support, underdeveloped adaptive feedback systems in blended contexts, limited modelling of writing development as an evolving process.
- [ ] The project does not overclaim that AI replaces assessment.
- [ ] The project states that current systems often fail to connect: behaviour, text quality, feedback, revision, pedagogy.
- [ ] The study explains why your context (third-year EFL students) is relevant and meaningful.

**C. Aim, objectives, and research questions**
- [ ] There is one main aim.
- [ ] There are clear, limited, well-scoped objectives.
- [ ] Every objective leads to at least one method or analysis.
- [ ] Every research question can be answered with the data you actually collect.
- [ ] No research question is too vague to operationalize.
- [ ] No objective depends on data you do not have.
- [ ] Objectives cover: designing the adaptive system, collecting Moodle process data, assessing writing products, integrating analytics and writing data, generating adaptive feedback, evaluating revision and improvement.
- [ ] Research questions cover: how students engage with the writing process, how process relates to product, how learner profiles can be identified, which factors predict writing improvement, how adaptive feedback supports development.
- [ ] The AI-related questions are proportionate to the study and do not overextend the thesis.

**D. Conceptual framework**
- [ ] The conceptual framework has clearly defined constructs.
- [ ] Constructs are grouped logically and not mixed randomly.
- [ ] The framework distinguishes: process constructs, product constructs, behavioural constructs, pedagogical response constructs.
- [ ] The conceptual framework includes: academic writing development, self-regulated learning, learning analytics, formative/adaptive feedback, AI-supported diagnosis.
- [ ] The framework makes causal or interpretive links explicit: process influences product, analytics diagnose needs, feedback influences revision, revision influences growth.
- [ ] The framework includes both observable and latent constructs.
- [ ] Observable constructs: time-on-task, revision frequency, feedback views, help-seeking, rubric scores, text indicators.
- [ ] Latent constructs: argument competence, cohesion competence, self-regulated revision, linguistic control.
- [ ] The framework shows how latent constructs are inferred from evidence.
- [ ] The framework is visually representable as a diagram.
- [ ] The framework makes clear where teacher agency exists.
- [ ] The framework makes clear where AI support exists.

**E. Theoretical grounding**
- [ ] Academic writing theory is explicitly used to justify: argumentation, cohesion, organization, lexical development, academic style.
- [ ] Self-regulated learning theory is explicitly used to justify: planning, monitoring, revision, reflection, help-seeking.
- [ ] Feedback theory is explicitly used to justify: feedback uptake, feedforward, dialogic feedback, teacher guidance.
- [ ] Learning analytics theory is explicitly used to justify: behavioural trace data, participation patterns, learner profiling, evidence-based intervention.
- [ ] Evidence-centered design or equivalent assessment logic is explicitly used to justify: observable evidence, interpretation, instructional decision.
- [ ] Each theoretical domain is actually used in the methodology and not only mentioned in Chapter 2.

**F. Research design**
- [ ] The research design is clearly named and justified.
- [ ] The design fits the aims (case study / intervention-based / blended assessment design).
- [ ] The design includes: task, data capture, feedback, revision, re-evaluation.
- [ ] The design clearly distinguishes: pre-test / baseline, intervention phase, revision phase, post-intervention evaluation.
- [ ] The design explains whether it is exploratory, descriptive, predictive, or interventionist.
- [ ] The design makes clear what counts as evidence of improvement.
- [ ] The unit of analysis is clearly defined: student, draft, task, revision episode.
- [ ] The design makes clear how many drafts per student are analysed.
- [ ] The design makes clear over what period data are collected.

**G. Context, participants, and sampling**
- [ ] The institutional context is described clearly.
- [ ] The course context is described clearly.
- [ ] The writing tasks belong naturally to the curriculum.
- [ ] The participants are defined by: year level, language background, course, institution.
- [ ] Inclusion and exclusion criteria are clear.
- [ ] The sample size is realistic for the chosen analyses.
- [ ] The limitations of sample size for AI models are acknowledged.
- [ ] The study avoids pretending the sample is larger or more generalizable than it is.

**H. Writing task design**
- [ ] Each writing task is fully specified.
- [ ] Task type is identified: introduction, body paragraph, full essay, argumentative paragraph.
- [ ] Each task includes: instructions, prompt, expected length, expected rhetorical structure, rubric access, support materials.
- [ ] The task aligns with the construct being assessed.
- [ ] The task is suitable for EFL university students.
- [ ] The task is demanding enough to generate meaningful process data.
- [ ] The task is not so complex that performance becomes uninterpretable.

**I. Rubric design**
- [ ] The rubric is analytic, not vague or overly holistic.
- [ ] Each rubric criterion has a clear construct definition.
- [ ] Each scale point is interpretable and distinct.
- [ ] The rubric dimensions align with academic writing theory.
- [ ] Rubric scores can be used statistically.
- [ ] Rubric levels are appropriate for EFL learners.
- [ ] The rubric includes enough dimensions to diagnose writing meaningfully without becoming unmanageable.
- [ ] Rubric dimensions include: task achievement, organization, argumentation, cohesion, lexical resource, grammar accuracy, academic style, mechanics.
- [ ] The rubric is stable across drafts and tasks.
- [ ] The rubric is applied consistently.
- [ ] Rater consistency or scoring reliability is considered.

**J. Process variables and Moodle analytics**
- [ ] Process data are defined before collection.
- [ ] Every process variable has: name, definition, source, measurement logic, pedagogical meaning.
- [ ] Core process variables: assignment views, rubric views, resource access, first access delay, time-on-task, revision frequency, feedback views, help-seeking messages, reflection participation.
- [ ] Process variables represent real educational processes, not just clicks.
- [ ] Variables are interpretable within SRL theory.
- [ ] Variables are not redundant.
- [ ] The difference between process data and engagement data is clear.

**K. Help-seeking and interaction data**
- [ ] Help-seeking is clearly operationalized.
- [ ] You distinguish: procedural help-seeking, conceptual help-seeking, language help-seeking, feedback clarification.
- [ ] Help-seeking is treated as a meaningful learning variable, not anecdotal information.
- [ ] Message data are coded with a transparent scheme.
- [ ] The coding scheme is described and replicable.
- [ ] Help-seeking is integrated into the adaptive model, not left separate.

**L. Text analytics and computational indicators**
- [ ] The project explains why text analytics are needed in addition to rubric scores.
- [ ] Each text variable is defined clearly.
- [ ] Each text variable has a computational procedure.
- [ ] Core text indicators: word count, lexical diversity (TTR or similar), cohesion index, error density, average sentence length, optional academic vocabulary index.
- [ ] Each text indicator has pedagogical meaning.
- [ ] Text indicators are not confused with rubric scores.
- [ ] Text indicators are used to complement, not replace, human assessment.

**M. Threshold system**
- [ ] Thresholds exist for all key variables.
- [ ] Thresholds are justified by: task expectations, rubric structure, writing theory, SRL theory, pilot calibration.
- [ ] Thresholds are not arbitrary.
- [ ] Thresholds are not claimed as universal truths.
- [ ] Thresholds are explicitly described as task-sensitive and context-sensitive.
- [ ] Threshold bands are interpretable: low / risk, moderate / watch, strong / satisfactory.
- [ ] Thresholds are defined for: word count, time-on-task, rubric views, revision frequency, criterion scores, error density, cohesion index, feedback uptake, help-seeking.
- [ ] The system allows threshold editing after pilot analysis.

**N. Rule-based interpretation layer**
- [ ] Rules exist and are explicit.
- [ ] Rules are written in a consistent format: condition, interpretation, feedback template, onsite intervention.
- [ ] Rules cover: product issues, process issues, engagement issues.
- [ ] Rule logic is transparent enough for a teacher to understand.
- [ ] Rule logic is simple enough to implement computationally.
- [ ] Rule logic is strong enough to support adaptive pedagogy.
- [ ] Product rules: low organization + low cohesion, weak argumentation, high error density, low lexical diversity.
- [ ] Process rules: no revision, low rubric consultation, feedback viewed but not used, short time-on-task, help-seeking after difficulty.
- [ ] Engagement rules: low participation, high effort + low quality, low engagement + acceptable quality.
- [ ] Rules are pedagogically grounded, not merely computational.

**O. AI output layer**
- [ ] AI outputs are explicitly defined as learner-state variables.
- [ ] The AI outputs are distinct from raw data.
- [ ] The AI outputs are usable by the rule layer.
- [ ] Core AI outputs: learner profile, predicted improvement, predicted score, argument competence probability, cohesion competence probability, self-regulated revision competence, forethought risk, feedback uptake risk, revision depth state.
- [ ] Each AI output has a clear interpretation.
- [ ] Each AI output corresponds to at least one pedagogical rule.

**P. Clustering**
- [ ] Clustering variables are clearly selected.
- [ ] Variables are standardized before clustering.
- [ ] Number of clusters is justified.
- [ ] Cluster labels are pedagogically interpretable.
- [ ] Clustering is used for profiling, not overclaimed as causal explanation.
- [ ] Possible outputs: strategic writer, engaged but developing writer, effortful but struggling writer, disengaged learner, fragile regulator.
- [ ] Cluster interpretation is based on actual cluster centers, not intuition alone.

**Q. Random Forest**
- [ ] Target variable is clearly defined: total score, score gain, probability of improvement.
- [ ] Predictor variables are clearly listed.
- [ ] Data leakage is avoided.
- [ ] The model output includes: prediction, feature importance.
- [ ] Feature importance is interpreted educationally.
- [ ] Random Forest is used to support prioritization, not replace pedagogy.
- [ ] The thesis does not overclaim predictive power if the sample is small.

**R. Bayesian modelling**
- [ ] Latent competencies are clearly defined.
- [ ] Observable indicators are correctly linked to latent competencies.
- [ ] States are manageable: low, medium, high.
- [ ] Priors are defined or justified.
- [ ] Posterior probabilities are interpretable.
- [ ] Bayesian modelling is kept simple enough for the thesis.
- [ ] It is used for competence inference, not as a vague technical ornament.
- [ ] Suggested latent nodes: argument structure, cohesion/coherence, linguistic complexity/control, self-regulated revision.
- [ ] Bayesian outputs are actually used by the rule table.

**S. Feedback template library**
- [ ] Every likely learner state maps to a feedback template.
- [ ] Feedback templates are: short, actionable, pedagogically clear, non-technical for students.
- [ ] Templates exist for: planning support, argument support, cohesion support, grammar support, vocabulary support, revision support, feedback uptake support, motivational support, metacognitive extension.
- [ ] Template wording matches the student’s actual problem.
- [ ] Templates can be combined when multiple rules are activated.

**T. Final pedagogical decision layer**
- [ ] The system clearly separates: AI diagnosis, rule selection, final pedagogical decision.
- [ ] Teacher review is built in.
- [ ] The teacher can: approve feedback, edit feedback, override the system, add comments.
- [ ] Final decisions remain pedagogically supervised.
- [ ] The system does not send high-stakes feedback blindly.

**U. Revision stage**
- [ ] Revision is tracked explicitly.
- [ ] Revision is distinguished as: no revision, surface revision, meaning-level revision.
- [ ] The student receives a real opportunity to revise.
- [ ] The revision stage is central to the study, not symbolic.

**V. Growth analysis across drafts**
- [ ] Growth is measured consistently across drafts.
- [ ] Growth measures include: rubric score gain, argument improvement, cohesion improvement, lexical development, error reduction, quality of revision.
- [ ] Growth is compared against the feedback focus.
- [ ] The study checks whether the system improved the targeted weakness, not just overall score.

**W. Core statistical analyses**
- [ ] Descriptive statistics are included.
- [ ] Product analysis is included.
- [ ] Text analytics are included.
- [ ] Correlation analysis is included.
- [ ] Growth analysis is included.
- [ ] Advanced AI models do not replace the basic analyses.
- [ ] The study remains methodologically strong even if advanced models are simplified.

**X. Technical implementation**
- [ ] Data pipeline works end-to-end.
- [ ] Input files are structured and documented.
- [ ] Missing values are handled.
- [ ] Indicators are computed correctly.
- [ ] Thresholds are applied correctly.
- [ ] Rules are triggered correctly.
- [ ] AI outputs are generated correctly.
- [ ] Feedback templates are selected correctly.
- [ ] Results are exportable.
- [ ] The system can produce one student diagnosis card.
- [ ] The system can process a full class dataset.
- [ ] Logs and results are stored clearly for audit and analysis.

**Y. UI/UX and teacher usability**
- [ ] Teacher dashboard is clear and not overloaded.
- [ ] Student view is simple and non-technical.
- [ ] Teacher sees: process evidence, product evidence, AI outputs, rules triggered, selected feedback, onsite support suggestion.
- [ ] Student sees: strengths, weaknesses, next steps, revision goal.
- [ ] The interface reduces workload instead of adding complexity.

**Z. Ethics, privacy, and transparency**
- [ ] Student consent is addressed.
- [ ] Data privacy is protected.
- [ ] Only authorized users can see student analytics.
- [ ] The system is explainable.
- [ ] Students are not treated as opaque machine labels.
- [ ] Teachers can justify every feedback decision.

**AA. Thesis writing and reporting**
- [ ] The thesis clearly explains the architecture step by step.
- [ ] Every major table and figure is included.
- [ ] The conceptual framework matches the methodology.
- [ ] The methodology matches the results chapter.
- [ ] The results chapter reports both analytics and pedagogy.
- [ ] The discussion chapter interprets findings educationally, not just technically.
- [ ] Essential visuals: conceptual framework diagram, system architecture diagram, threshold table, rule table, AI output table, feedback template table, workflow/pipeline figure.

**AB. Defense readiness**
- [ ] You can explain in one sentence: what the project solves, why AI is included, why the teacher remains central.
- [ ] You can defend: why these thresholds, why these variables, why these rules, why clustering, why Random Forest, why Bayesian modelling, why your system is not overengineered.
- [ ] You can explain how one real student moved through the full cycle: data → thresholds → rules → AI outputs → feedback → revision → growth.
- [ ] You can explain what would still be valid even if one AI component were removed.

**Final master standard**
Your project is ready only if it satisfies this full chain:
Clear problem → coherent framework → valid variables → justified thresholds → transparent rules → meaningful AI outputs → teacher-supervised decisions → revision opportunity → measurable growth → interpretable contribution

**Non-negotiable principle**
The project must always respect this logic: AI supports diagnosis; pedagogy controls intervention. That is the deepest standard your project must satisfy.
